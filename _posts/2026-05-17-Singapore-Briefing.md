## THE GROUND PICTURE

Living and working in Singapore is currently defined by a sharp contrast between long-term infrastructure stability and immediate financial and professional anxiety. The government is committing significant resources to physical renewals, such as the **Home Improvement Programme** for 18,000 HDB flats and the completion of the **Circle Line** MRT loop. These projects aim to maintain the quality of the living environment as the population ages and the city grows.

However, the material reality for many professionals is one of tightening margins and shifting job security. While the **Singdollar** remains strong against regional currencies, global conflicts are driving up the cost of essentials like fuel and electricity. At the same time, the rapid push toward **Artificial Intelligence** is no longer a distant prospect but a core part of national policy that is beginning to reshape job descriptions and hiring expectations across the IT and service sectors.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost of living in Singapore is being heavily influenced by the ongoing conflict in the **Strait of Hormuz**. This global chokepoint has caused a significant rise in fuel prices, which is filtering down into transport costs and grocery bills. The government has responded with targeted support, such as a $200 cash payout for platform workers and increased school bus subsidies, but broader inflationary pressures remain a concern for the middle class.

In the housing market, policy is shifting to prioritize first-time buyers and long-term residency. The **Minimum Occupation Period (MOP)** for **Executive Condominiums** has been doubled to 10 years to curb speculative buying. While private home sales saw a recent jump in areas like **Tengah** and **Bayshore**, there are signs of financial strain in the luxury segment, with at least one forced sale per month occurring in condos due to unpaid maintenance fees.

The banking sector is pivoting its strategy as interest margins shrink. **DBS**, **OCBC**, and **UOB** are increasingly focusing on wealth management to drive profits. For the average consumer, this means a market where credit is becoming more expensive and banks are more selective. Retailers are also feeling the pinch, with some shifting from traditional loyalty points to real-time behavior tracking to maintain sales in a cautious spending environment.

The core dynamic is a cost stack pushed upward by global energy volatility and a housing market undergoing structural cooling.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The job market is currently in a state of high-speed transition. The **Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** has announced plans to train 40,000 tech workers in AI by 2029 to automate coding and build agentic systems. This move is intended to keep Singapore competitive, but it coincides with a period of high burnout, with reports suggesting that 61% of workers feel exhausted.

Retrenchments are visible in specific sectors as companies reorganize. **H&M** is moving its regional headquarters from Singapore to Malaysia, and firms like **JLL** have conducted restructuring exercises. In the IT sector, there is a growing gap between the demand for AI-related skills and the availability of traditional roles. Some graduates are reporting difficulty finding work despite having multiple internships, while others are seeing starting salaries stagnate around $3,000.

The government is using **Jobs Transformation Maps** to signal which roles are most at risk. In the accounting and finance sectors, roles like tax associates and audit assistants are being redesigned to focus on data governance rather than manual entry. To support this, **Workforce Singapore** is promoting the **Career Conversion Programme** to help mid-career professionals move into emerging roles like ESG specialists or AI hygiene controllers.

The core dynamic is a forced evolution of the workforce where AI proficiency is becoming a mandatory requirement for job security.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is directing its energy toward "Smart Nation 2.0," with a heavy emphasis on AI governance and digital safety. A new **Online Harms Agency** will begin operations in June to regulate digital spaces. There is also a clear focus on preventing "jobless growth" during the AI transition, led by the newly formed **Tripartite Jobs Council** and a proposed **Skills and Workforce Development Agency**.

Significant funding is being funneled into deep tech and sustainability. **SEEDS Capital** has set aside $150 million for co-investments in deep tech startups, and the **Ministry of National Development** is validating fees to ensure agencies have the legal backing to collect revenue for urban services. The government is also expanding healthcare initiatives, such as the **Home Improvement Programme** senior-friendly upgrades and trials for multi-cancer early detection tests.

Policy attention is also turning to the falling birth rate and the "sandwich class" of caregivers. The **Ministry of Manpower** is reviewing return-to-work paths for those who have been ill or injured, and there are ongoing discussions about introducing caregiving leave for parents. The government is deferring reviews of political salaries until the economic impact of the Middle East conflict is clearer, signaling a cautious approach to public spending.

The core direction is the creation of a highly regulated, AI-enabled economy with a strengthened social safety net for the vulnerable.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore’s regional position is being redefined by its relationship with Malaysia. The **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** and the **RTS Link** are moving toward completion, which will likely treat the two territories as a single economic destination. However, Malaysia is becoming a more aggressive competitor for corporate headquarters, as seen with **H&M’s** relocation to Kuala Lumpur.

In the wider ASEAN region, Singapore is positioning itself as a "trusted partner" for trade rules, particularly with the UK and New Zealand. While scam networks are shifting their operations from Southeast Asia to Sri Lanka following regional crackdowns, Singapore remains a primary target for sophisticated deepfake fraud. Regional infrastructure projects, like Thailand’s proposed land bridge, are also being monitored for their potential to bypass Singapore’s traditional shipping dominance.

The **Strait of Malacca** remains a critical asset, but Indonesia and Malaysia have firmly stated they will not allow unilateral changes to its status, such as new tolls. Singapore is also exploring clean-electricity trade with Indonesia, aiming for a year-end goal to start importing renewable energy. This regional integration is necessary for resource security but introduces new dependencies on the political stability of neighboring states.

The core dynamic is a shift toward deep economic integration with Johor while facing increased competition from regional neighbors for high-value corporate functions.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

The rivalry between the **United States** and **China** continues to dictate the boundaries of Singapore’s trade and technology sectors. While the recent summit between **Donald Trump** and **Xi Jinping** brought a temporary sense of stability, the lack of a major strategic breakthrough means Singaporean firms must continue to navigate tariff risks and supply chain diversification. Local chipmakers like **AEM** and tech giants like **Singtel** are directly affected by US restrictions on AI technology and Nvidia chips.

The **Iran war** and the resulting instability in the **Strait of Hormuz** are the most immediate global threats to the Singaporean household. Global oil prices have risen by roughly 60% since the conflict began, which is the primary driver of local electricity and transport price hikes. Singapore’s refusal to negotiate for safe passage through the Strait reinforces its commitment to international law but leaves the economy exposed to prolonged energy price shocks.
