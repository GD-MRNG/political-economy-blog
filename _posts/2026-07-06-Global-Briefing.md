# Executive Summary

**The Global Operating Picture**

The contemporary global operating picture is defined by the friction between institutional path dependency and the material realities of a multipolar distribution of power. While the Western-led financial and security architectures continue to command significant structural power—underpinned by the centrality of the US dollar and legacy alliance systems—their capacity to enforce universal compliance has diminished. Emerging and middle powers are increasingly operating within a framework of strategic hedging, refusing to commit to binary geopolitical alignments. This is not merely a diplomatic posture but a structural adaptation to a fragmented global economy, where security dependencies, resource endowments, and capital requirements are rarely aligned with a single external partner.

Beneath the surface of geopolitical competition lies a fundamental realignment of global industrial policy and trade flows. The transition from efficiency-driven supply chains to resilience- and security-oriented architectures is accelerating, driven by state-directed capital and regulatory interventions. This shift is characterized by a paradox: while political rhetoric emphasizes decoupling and de-risking, material interdependencies remain deep, leading to the rerouting of trade through intermediary jurisdictions rather than genuine reshoring. Consequently, global trade is becoming more complex, opaque, and transactionally expensive, shifting the burden of adjustment onto developing economies that lack the fiscal space to subsidize domestic industrial bases.

In the monetary domain, the institutionalization of parallel financial architectures is transitioning from a defensive contingency to an active structural feature of the global economy. Sanctions regimes and the freezing of sovereign assets have accelerated efforts by non-Western actors to develop alternative clearing, settlement, and payment systems. While these mechanisms do not yet pose an immediate threat to the liquidity and dominance of the US dollar in global reserves, they are successfully insulating localized trade corridors—particularly in energy and critical minerals—from Western regulatory jurisdiction. This fragmentation of the global financial plumbing reduces the efficacy of economic coercion as a tool of statecraft while increasing the systemic complexity of cross-border capital flows.

Finally, the multilateral governance framework is experiencing a profound legitimacy crisis, characterized by the paralysis of treaty-based organizations and the rise of minilateral and ad-hoc coalitions. The inability of legacy institutions to resolve systemic crises—ranging from sovereign debt distress in the Global South to maritime security disruptions—has prompted a shift toward transactional, issue-specific alignments. Actors across the Islamic world, Africa, and Latin America are leveraging this institutional fluidity to negotiate better terms of engagement with competing great powers, treating international law not as a fixed set of rules but as a site of ongoing contestation and renegotiation.

**Key Strategic Shifts**

* **Institutionalization of parallel financial plumbing.** Non-Western states are accelerating the deployment of local-currency settlement mechanisms and alternative messaging systems to bypass Western-dominated financial infrastructure. This shift is transitioning from a sanctions-evasion tactic to a permanent structural feature of bilateral trade, particularly within the BRICS+ framework and Eurasian energy corridors. While it does not displace the US dollar's global reserve status, it permanently reduces the reach of Western regulatory oversight over strategic commodity flows.

* **Proliferation of strategic hedging among middle powers.** Middle powers across the Middle East, Southeast Asia, and Latin America are systematically resisting pressure to adopt binary alignments, choosing instead to optimize their security and economic relationships independently. By maintaining security ties with the West while deepening trade and infrastructure integration with China and Eurasia, these actors are maximizing their sovereign autonomy. This dynamic is decelerating the formation of rigid, bloc-based alliances and forcing great powers to engage in highly transactional diplomacy.

* **Fragmentation of global supply chains through intermediary nodes.** The pursuit of supply chain resilience is driving a structural rerouting of trade through "connector" economies rather than achieving genuine domestic self-sufficiency. Countries such as Vietnam, Mexico, and India are acting as critical intermediaries, importing intermediate goods from China and exporting finished products to Western markets. This trend increases transaction costs and supply chain complexity while preserving the underlying material dependencies that political actors seek to sever.

* **State-directed industrial policy as the primary driver of economic competition.** The return of aggressive state intervention, subsidies, and tariff barriers in major economies is displacing market-driven globalization as the organizing principle of international trade. This shift is creating structural overcapacity in green technologies and advanced manufacturing, leading to retaliatory trade measures and localized protectionism. Developing economies unable to compete with the fiscal subsidies of the United States, the European Union, or China are increasingly marginalized, exacerbating global wealth disparities.
