## THE GROUND PICTURE

The material reality of living in Singapore is currently defined by a contradiction between rising personal income and growing systemic instability. While the **Ministry of Manpower (MOM)** reports that real wages grew by 4% in 2025 as inflation eased, this financial gain is being offset by a "fossil premium" on energy and goods caused by the ongoing war in Iran. You are likely feeling the benefit of a stronger **Singapore Dollar**, which has hit record highs against regional currencies, yet your long-term security is being challenged by a rapid, state-led push to integrate Artificial Intelligence into every sector of the economy.

The government is signaling that the era of stable, predictable career paths is ending. Policy attention has shifted toward managing the "AI chill" in the job market and preparing the population for a "super-aged" society. While the physical infrastructure of the city continues to expand with projects like the **Cross Island Line** and the redevelopment of the **East Coast Seafood Centre**, the social infrastructure is being rewired to handle higher levels of caregiver stress and digital disruption.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

Economic conditions are stabilizing at a high price floor. Core inflation fell to 1.4% in April, which is lower than previous forecasts, but the cost of daily essentials remains a primary concern for most households. To manage this, the **FairPrice Group** has frozen prices on over 500 essential items until late 2026 and doubled **CHAS** discounts for lower-income residents. However, these subsidies compete with rising electricity bills and fuel surcharges as the energy crisis persists. **Grab** has extended its fuel surcharges, and analysts warn of a potential spike in utility tariffs later this year.

The housing market is showing signs of a split. While private residential rents are beginning to flatten due to a steady supply of new units, the cost of entry for the "sandwich class" remains high. The government is responding by increasing the supply of **Build-To-Order (BTO)** flats through 2027 and tightening rules for **Executive Condominiums (EC)** to prioritize first-time buyers. Meanwhile, the **Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS)** is actively clawing back properties from investors who used sham deals to avoid the **Additional Buyer’s Stamp Duty (ABSD)**, signaling a zero-tolerance approach to property speculation.

Financial independence is becoming a more expensive goal. A recent study indicates that residents now believe they need at least $1 million in savings to retire comfortably, with many aiming to reach this target in their 40s. This drive for higher savings is occurring just as the **Central Provident Fund (CPF)** interest rates remain unchanged, forcing a greater reliance on private investments. The core dynamic is a race between moderate wage growth and the rising cost of long-term financial security.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The job market is undergoing a structural shift driven by the **Economic Strategy Review (ESR)**. While the finance and IT sectors recorded the highest salary increases recently, these same industries are facing the most significant anxiety regarding AI displacement. Nearly half of all finance workers now express fear about their job security. The government is countering this by launching the **AI-Ready SG: Skills Lab** and providing over one million AI training places through **NTUC**. The goal is to prevent "jobless growth," where the economy expands but local employment does not.

Manufacturing is seeing a visible migration of production to neighboring countries. Companies like **Gardenia** and **Yeo Hiap Seng** have recently moved production to Malaysia to manage costs, leading to local retrenchments. Conversely, high-value sectors like semiconductor manufacturing and wealth management are expanding. **Mizuho** and other global banks are increasing their headcount in Singapore to support regional growth, even as they automate junior-level roles.

Workplace culture is also in flux. There is a persistent stigma against flexible work arrangements despite government efforts to normalize them. Burnout is a significant issue, with 61% of workers reporting exhaustion, particularly in high-pressure fields like law and healthcare. This has led to a rise in employees seeking extended no-pay leave and a "return-to-work" review by **MOM** for those recovering from illness. The core dynamic is a transition from traditional labor-intensive roles to high-skill, AI-augmented positions amidst high levels of worker fatigue.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is directing massive resources into three specific areas: AI integration, family resilience, and the "longevity economy." A $350 million longevity challenge has been launched to tackle cognitive decline in the aging population. In the digital space, the **Ministry of Communications and Information** is moving beyond simple regulation to active intervention, launching the **Be Screen Smart** portal to help parents manage their children's digital habits. This reflects a broader policy shift toward protecting social cohesion in a highly digitized environment.

Specific industry support is being funneled through **Workforce Singapore (WSG)** and **Enterprise Singapore (EnterpriseSG)**. The **Career Conversion Programme (CCP)** is being used to reskill mid-career professionals in the built environment, cleaning, and food sectors. New regulatory bodies, such as the **Veterinary Council**, are being established to professionalize niche industries. The government is also intervening in the media and tech landscape, passing laws that require approval for major media acquisitions and regulating "blind boxes" and trading cards to protect consumers.

Infrastructure spending remains a priority to maintain Singapore’s status as a global hub. The **Land Transport Authority (LTA)** is awarding billion-dollar contracts for the **Changi Southern Corridor** to support the future **Terminal 5**. At the same time, the government is preparing for the next pandemic by reviewing child protection measures and hospital decision-making workflows. The core direction is the aggressive modernization of both the workforce and the physical city to withstand external shocks.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its integration with its neighbors while navigating regional instability. The **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** is nearing completion, which is expected to change how businesses operate across the border. This is complemented by the **ASEAN Digital Economy Framework Agreement**, set to be signed in late 2026, which will standardize digital trade across the region. These moves are intended to make the region more attractive as a single market, even as individual countries like Malaysia implement austerity measures.

Competition for talent and status is intensifying. Hong Kong is actively challenging Singapore for the title of the region's best airport and top wealth hub, recently overtaking Switzerland in offshore wealth. In response, Singapore is strengthening bilateral ties with Vietnam through the **Vietnam-Singapore Industrial Park** and food security pacts. However, regional security remains a concern, with the **Strait of Malacca** and the **South China Sea** seeing increased military patrols and diplomatic friction over cancelled defense deals.

Infrastructure in the region is becoming more interconnected. A new taxi scheme for Johor and the expansion of **Grab** rides to Malaysia are making cross-border travel more accessible for residents. However, Malaysia’s introduction of a new **MyKad** and stricter food import regulations starting in June may create temporary friction at the checkpoints. The core dynamic is a push for deeper regional economic unity to hedge against global trade volatility.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

The war in Iran is the most significant global force hitting Singaporean doorsteps. It has created a "fossil premium" that keeps electricity and transport costs high despite easing general inflation. The **Strait of Hormuz** disruptions are forcing shipping lines to reroute, which impacts the arrival of everything from coffee beans to industrial chemicals. Singapore has responded by guaranteeing sufficient **Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG)** supplies through 2026, but retail prices remain sensitive to global shocks.

The US-China tech rivalry is also landing locally through increased scrutiny of high-risk tech shipments. Singapore is under pressure to implement AI-led spot checks on exports to ensure compliance with international sanctions. Simultaneously, the "onshoring" of manufacturing in the US is creating new opportunities for Singaporean firms that can provide high-tech components. The **Shangri-La Dialogue** held in Singapore recently highlighted these tensions, with the US emphasizing military presence while Southeast Asian nations called for more diplomatic summits.
