## THE GROUND PICTURE

Singapore is navigating a period of transition where global relief and local friction meet. The signing of the US-Iran peace deal has reopened the Strait of Hormuz, bringing down global oil prices and boosting local stock markets. Yet, this external relief has not fully eased the immediate financial pressures felt by residents on the ground.

Retrenchments rose in the first quarter of 2026, driven primarily by corporate restructuring and reorganization. This job market tightening is hitting degree holders and older workers the hardest, even as overall employment growth slows down. At the same time, everyday costs like insurance premiums and waste collection fees continue to climb, forcing households to adjust their budgets.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost of living in Singapore remains high despite the drop in global oil prices following the US-Iran peace deal. Local utility bills and fuel prices remain elevated, and household refuse collection fees are set to rise on July 1, 2026. To help families cope, the government is releasing a new round of **Community Development Council (CDC) Vouchers** on June 11, 2026, while **FairPrice Group** has frozen prices on 500 daily essentials.

In the property market, buyers are showing caution. New private home sales fell to a three-month low in May 2026, and resale condominiums are staying on the market longer as buyers take their time and sellers hold out for high prices. Meanwhile, healthcare costs are rising, with changes to **Integrated Shield Plan (IP)** riders increasing co-payment limits and prompting residents to re-evaluate their insurance coverage.

The financial sector is adjusting to new investment realities. The **Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS)** is launching an over-the-counter gold clearing system and central bank vaulting services by the end of 2026 to build a trusted tokenized gold hub. However, local banks face uneven pressures, with **DBS** facing downgrades from analysts due to exposure to new Chinese regulations on outbound investment.

*Core dynamic: Local household budgets are squeezed by rising domestic service and healthcare costs even as global energy markets stabilize.*

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The job market is softening, with the **Ministry of Manpower (MOM)** reporting a rise in retrenchments and a dip in job vacancies for the first quarter of 2026. Companies are dialing back general hiring, but they remain willing to pay premium wages for specialized skills in artificial intelligence and soft skills. This trend is visible in the tech sector, where **Shopee** laid off hundreds of developers to pivot toward AI, while demand for **Forward Deployed Engineers (FDEs)** has risen sharply.

Despite the cautious hiring environment, Singapore continues to attract regional headquarters and research facilities. The AI work platform **Monday.com** chose Singapore as its second Asia-Pacific headquarters, and AI hardware firm **Plaud** plans to establish its first regional research and development hub here. However, white-collar workers face intense competition as automation begins to reshape traditional roles in legal, accounting, and administrative services.

*Core dynamic: General hiring is contracting as employers aggressively shift headcount budgets toward specialized artificial intelligence roles.*

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

Prime Minister Lawrence Wong has emphasized a pragmatic approach to governance, focusing on keeping policies that work and changing those that do not. To support workers through economic restructuring, the government announced the formation of the **Tripartite Jobs Council** and plans to establish a new **Skills and Workforce Development Agency** later this year. The **Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** has also partnered with **Microsoft** to deepen local AI safety initiatives and establish a registry for AI agents to ensure workplace accountability.

Infrastructure and regulatory updates are also moving forward. The **Urban Redevelopment Authority (URA)** is scaling up non-invasive underground detection technology to map utilities, which has already saved millions in construction costs. On the regulatory front, the government is tightening rules on electric vehicles up to 3,000kg by extending revised driving license requirements starting June 15, 2026, and is introducing new **PayNow** security features to prevent digital scams.

*Core dynamic: The state is actively intervening to build safety nets for the AI transition while tightening digital security and transport regulations.*

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its economic ties with its immediate neighbors to maintain its competitive edge. Planning for the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** is nearing completion, and Singapore has passed laws to handle security and operational incidents on the upcoming **Rapid Transit System (RTS) Link**. Additionally, local digital integration is expanding, allowing Singapore residents to open **OCBC Malaysia** business accounts remotely using **Singpass** for authentication.

However, regional political and economic shifts require close monitoring. In Malaysia, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim faces a crucial test of political stability in upcoming state polls, while the country is also implementing a social media ban for users under 16. In Indonesia, fiscal concerns and currency volatility have prompted central bank interventions, reminding businesses of the financial fragility within the wider ASEAN region.

*Core dynamic: Singapore is accelerating physical and digital integration with Malaysia while navigating broader economic volatility across Southeast Asia.*

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global geopolitical tensions and trade controls are directly impacting Singapore's technology and resource sectors. The United States government has blocked foreign access to advanced AI models from firms like **Anthropic** and is taking steps to halt high-end **Nvidia** AI chip shipments to Chinese subsidiaries overseas, including those in Malaysia. These export controls complicate supply chains for Singapore's semiconductor industry, which currently produces one-tenth of the world's microchips.

The resolution of the US-Iran conflict has brought immediate relief to global shipping lanes, allowing vessels to transit the Strait of Hormuz without paying premiums. This has stabilized global trade routes, but local marine fuel sales in Singapore have dipped as cheaper bunker options emerge in China. Singapore is also under pressure to adopt AI-led checks on high-risk technology shipments to comply with international trade security standards.
