## THE GROUND PICTURE

Singapore is navigating a dual reality of high-tech growth and intense local cost pressures. While the artificial intelligence boom has pushed manufacturing activity to an eight-year high, everyday living costs remain elevated. 

At the same time, the job market is tightening. Major technology firms like **Microsoft**, **Lazada**, and **TikTok** have executed layoffs, and local banks have virtually frozen headcount growth. 

In the policy space, the government is tightening regulations around digital safety, property transactions, and professional conduct. High-profile legal proceedings, including the **Law Society of Singapore**'s disciplinary hearing against Workers' Party chief Pritam Singh, are scheduled for August.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost stack for Singapore residents remains heavy, driven by structural increases in essential services. **Electricity tariffs** have reached record highs, prompting retail energy providers to offer temporary discounts. Healthcare costs are also rising, with insurance premiums for **Integrated Shield Plan (IP)** riders increasing as the **Ministry of Health (MOH)** implements co-payment changes to manage medical inflation.

Consumer spending is showing signs of caution. While retail sales grew for the fourth consecutive month in May, the 3% year-on-year increase missed market forecasts, and sales actually declined when excluding motor vehicles. Meanwhile, the property market is cooling slightly. Private home price growth slowed to its lowest pace in seven quarters, and **Housing & Development Board (HDB)** resale prices fell for the second consecutive quarter.

To help households manage these persistent pressures, the government distributed $500 in **CDC vouchers** in June. However, high-end lifestyle costs, such as gym memberships and dental fees, continue to strain middle-class budgets.

**Core dynamic**: Household budgets are being squeezed by record-high utility and healthcare costs while retail and property markets show signs of cooling.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The employment landscape is undergoing a structural shift as companies adjust to high interest rates and technological disruption. Tech sector workers face a challenging market, marked by **Microsoft**'s global cut of 4,800 jobs, alongside local layoffs at **Lazada** and **TikTok**. Local banks are also exercising extreme caution, with headcount growing by a mere 0.005% over the past year.

Conversely, the hardware side of technology is thriving. Singapore's manufacturing **Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI)** hit an eight-year high of 51.3, driven by surging global demand for AI-related semiconductors. This has created a talent split, where hardware and infrastructure roles remain in high demand while software, services, and entry-level roles face consolidation.

To manage senior headcount costs, employers are increasingly hiring fractional executives rather than full-time directors. Furthermore, the **Jobs Transformation Maps (JTM)** published by the government indicate that junior roles in finance, accounting, and administrative functions are facing significant automation risks.

**Core dynamic**: A sharp divergence has emerged between a booming hardware manufacturing sector and a consolidating tech services and banking job market.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The Singapore government is actively steering the economy toward structured AI adoption while building safety nets against digital harms. The **Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** and **Enterprise Singapore** are subsidizing AI tools for small businesses, while **NTUC** has committed to providing over one million AI training places. To coordinate these efforts, the government will establish a new **Skills and Workforce Development Agency (SWDA)** later this year.

Regulatory oversight is tightening across multiple sectors. The **Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS)** has proposed a new framework to govern autonomous AI agents in financial services to prevent rapid, unmanaged market transactions. Additionally, the **Singapore Police Force** has launched a new **Cyber Command** to intercept scammers using local SIM cards and digital channels.

Infrastructure planning also remains a priority. The **National Heritage Board (NHB)** and **PUB** are assessing the historical significance of the 135-year-old **Bukit Timah Waterworks** to guide future land use, while preparatory works for the **Long Island** coastal protection project are scheduled to begin at East Coast Park by the end of 2026.

**Core direction**: The state is accelerating national AI readiness while aggressively expanding regulatory frameworks to police digital finance, cybersecurity, and urban development.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its economic and strategic ties with its immediate neighbors to secure supply chains and energy resources. Prime Minister Lawrence Wong and Indonesian President Prabowo Subianto recently reaffirmed their commitment to keeping the **Strait of Malacca** open and free. The two nations also signed a landmark carbon credits pact and agreements to facilitate cross-border trade for small and medium-sized enterprises.

In Malaysia, the development of the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** is nearing completion, with an official blueprint launch expected in the fourth quarter. Johor's economy grew by 8% due to a massive influx of data centre investments, though this rapid expansion has caused friction, with Johor's Chief Minister urging the federal government to resolve master plan delays.

This regional growth presents both opportunities and competition. While the JS-SEZ is expected to create 20,000 skilled jobs, Malaysian political coalitions are actively campaigning to attract 20,000 Malaysian professionals currently working in Singapore back to Johor by 2031.

**Core dynamic**: Singapore is integrating closer with Indonesia and Malaysia on green energy and trade, even as Johor emerges as a fast-growing competitor for tech infrastructure and talent.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global geopolitical tensions and supply chain realignments are directly impacting Singapore's domestic security and economy. The ongoing Middle East conflict has elevated the global security threat level, prompting the Singapore government to defer scheduled reviews of political salaries until the economic fallout is clearer. While global oil markets have temporarily absorbed supply losses, energy-hungry Asian markets remain highly vulnerable to shipping disruptions in the **Strait of Hormuz**.

The global race for advanced technology has also brought illicit activities to Singapore's shores. Local authorities recently seized a $42 million home and filed money laundering charges against a local tech CEO in connection with a massive international **Nvidia** chip smuggling and fraud case. This highlights how US-led export controls on critical technology are creating compliance and legal risks for Singapore-based firms.
