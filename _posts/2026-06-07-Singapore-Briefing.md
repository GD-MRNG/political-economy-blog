## THE GROUND PICTURE

The material reality for a professional in Singapore is currently defined by a transition toward an **AI-driven economy** amid persistent regional instability. While core inflation has eased to 1.4%, the cost of essential services and transport remains high. The government is actively intervening with direct household support and aggressive industry subsidies to manage this shift.

Job security is bifurcating. High-value roles in **Artificial Intelligence** and data centers are expanding as global firms like **OpenAI**, **Nvidia**, and **Anthropic** establish local hubs. Simultaneously, traditional manufacturing and back-office functions are facing pressure from automation or relocating to neighboring countries to lower costs.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

Economic conditions are stabilizing but remain expensive. The **Ministry of Trade and Industry (MTI)** reports that core inflation has fallen below forecasts, yet the "cost stack" for households is being pressured by external forces. The ongoing conflict in the Middle East has created a "fossil premium," keeping energy and fuel prices volatile. To offset these pressures, the government is distributing $500 in **CDC Vouchers** to all households, and **FairPrice Group** has frozen prices on 500 daily essentials through August 2026.

Housing and transport costs show mixed signals. **HDB** is increasing the supply of flats with shorter waiting times, and private residential resale prices have seen a slight decline. However, **Certificate of Entitlement (COE)** prices for cars and commercial vehicles remain at historic highs, with Category A premiums exceeding $126,000. For homeowners, the gap between Singapore and US interest rates is widening, which may influence mortgage strategies as the era of high interest on cash deposits begins to end.

The core dynamic is a cooling of general inflation being countered by structural high costs in transport and energy.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The labor market is undergoing a significant "job redesign" phase. Real wages grew by 4% in 2025, led by the finance and IT sectors. However, burnout is a documented reality for 61% of the workforce. In the IT sector, the arrival of **Anthropic** and the expansion of **Singtel’s** AI capabilities, supported by government funding, are creating high-value roles. Conversely, firms like **Gardenia** and **Yeo Hiap Seng** are moving production to Malaysia, leading to local retrenchments.

The **Ministry of Manpower (MOM)** and **NTUC** are signaling that "jobless growth" during the AI transition is a primary concern. **NTUC** plans to provide 1 million AI training places to help workers adapt. For IT professionals, the focus is shifting from general software engineering to specialized roles in **AI Ethics**, **Model Risk**, and **Data Management**. Competition for talent is also heating up in regional hubs like India, which may affect the availability of specialized foreign expertise in Singapore.

The core dynamic is a rapid replacement of traditional roles with AI-integrated positions, supported by heavy state-led retraining.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is steering Singapore toward becoming a global node for **AI and Sustainable Finance**. Significant policy energy is being directed through the **National AI Strategy** and the **Financial Sector Technology and Innovation Scheme (FSTI 3.0)**. A new **Skills and Workforce Development Agency** will be established later this year to centralize the upskilling effort. The government is also tightening digital safety, with the **Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** and **Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA)** actively blocking foreign online content that threatens social cohesion.

Infrastructure and healthcare are receiving long-term investment to manage an ageing population. The **Home Improvement Programme** is being expanded to 18,000 more flats, and the **Ministry of Health (MOH)** is shifting toward social care models for seniors. In transport, the government is co-funding essential bus services and preparing for the rollout of autonomous vehicles in **Punggol** by 2026. Regulatory focus is also increasing on "dark patterns" in online sales and the accountability of AI agents.

The core direction is the aggressive use of state grants and new agencies to de-risk the economy from AI disruption and demographic ageing.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore’s position is increasingly tied to the success of the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)**. This initiative aims to allow businesses to keep headquarters in Singapore while moving production to Johor to manage costs. The **RTS Link** and new cross-border taxi schemes are physical manifestations of this integration. However, regional competition is intensifying as Malaysia and Vietnam successfully attract semiconductor and data center investments that might previously have gone to Singapore.

Political shifts in the neighborhood are creating uncertainty for investors. In Indonesia, the market is reacting to President Prabowo’s tightening grip on the economy, leading to some capital outflows that have benefited Singapore’s wealth management sector. Meanwhile, Thailand and Malaysia are navigating internal political tensions that could affect bilateral trade agreements. Singapore continues to emphasize its role as a neutral, stable hub, recently reiterating its commitment to keeping the **Strait of Malacca** open for global trade.

The core dynamic is a move toward deep economic integration with Johor to maintain regional competitiveness against rising neighbors.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global trade tensions are landing directly on Singapore’s doorstep. The **Ministry of Trade and Industry** has warned that a potential 12.5% US tariff on exports could impact one-third of Singapore’s trade with the United States. This is driven by US probes into forced labor and supply chain origins. Additionally, the **US-China AI race** is forcing local firms to navigate conflicting regulatory environments, with the US restricting certain high-end chip shipments to subsidiaries in the region.

The volatility of global tech IPOs, such as **SpaceX** and **OpenAI**, is directly affecting local tech stocks and venture capital flows. As global investors seek "safe harbors" from the conflict in the Middle East and economic instability in Europe, Singapore is seeing a surge in capital inflows from the Gulf region. This influx of wealth supports the local banking sector, specifically **DBS**, **OCBC**, and **UOB**, but also contributes to the sustained high cost of luxury property and services.

## WHAT TO WATCH

The finalization of the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone** terms in early 2026.

The impact of the **US Trade Representative** hearings in July on Singapore’s export tariffs.

The implementation of new **MOH** insurance rider changes and their effect on private healthcare costs.

The success of **NTUC’s** AI-Ready workshops in reducing burnout and improving job placement.

The stability of the **Strait of Hormuz** and its direct effect on Singapore’s electricity and fuel prices.
