## THE GROUND PICTURE

Singapore's economy is showing strong headline growth, with GDP expanding by 5.7 percent in the second quarter of 2026. This growth is heavily concentrated in the manufacturing and semiconductor sectors, fueled by a global race to build artificial intelligence infrastructure. 

However, this tech-driven surge does not translate directly into everyday financial ease. Residents are navigating a heavier cost stack, marked by rising utility bills, high petrol prices, and changes to health insurance that increase out-of-pocket expenses. 

At the same time, the professional landscape is shifting. While the government continues to invest heavily in future-proofing infrastructure, companies are restructuring, and workers are facing higher productivity expectations driven by the rapid adoption of artificial intelligence tools.

---

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost of living in Singapore remains high, prompting both public and private interventions to ease the burden on households. The government brought forward the distribution of **Community Development Council (CDC) Vouchers** to June 2026, while **FairPrice Group** has frozen prices on over 500 daily essentials. **DBS Bank** and **POSB** have also introduced targeted discounts on basic groceries like rice and eggs to complement these efforts.

In the housing market, private home price growth moderated to 0.5 percent in the second quarter, and HDB resale prices fell for the second consecutive quarter. Despite this cooling, demand for premium public housing remains strong, with most buyers of million-dollar HDB flats paying no Cash Over Valuation in 2025. Meanwhile, the rental market remains firm, with non-landed private residential rents rising slightly.

Other components of the household cost stack are rising. Electricity tariffs are expected to increase, and global fuel price volatility linked to Middle East tensions is keeping petrol prices high. In healthcare, consumers are re-evaluating their coverage as **Ministry of Health (MOH)** policy changes to Integrated Shield Plan riders increase co-payment exposure.

The material reality for residents is a dual-speed economy where strong macroeconomic growth coexists with persistent, creeping pressures across essential household expenses.

---

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The employment market is undergoing structural adjustments, particularly within the technology and professional services sectors. The **Government Technology Agency (GovTech)** recently cut 93 roles in a restructuring exercise, though the **Public Service Division** clarified this is not part of a wider public service retrenchment. In the private sector, new graduates are increasingly accepting contract roles as a starting point, while legal professionals report high rates of burnout, with one in three considering leaving the industry.

The widespread adoption of artificial intelligence is changing workplace dynamics. Gen Z workers are actively using AI tools to manage their workloads, but this has led employers to raise their productivity expectations. For those facing retrenchment, legal guidance has clarified that employers can still enforce non-compete clauses even after an employee has been laid off.

Lower-wage sectors are seeing targeted policy improvements. The **Ministry of Manpower (MOM)** announced that outsourced workers under the **Progressive Wage Model (PWM)** in sectors like cleaning, security, and landscaping will see their baseline annual leave increase from seven to ten days by 2029. The Progressive Wage Model is also expanding to cover the pest management sector, bringing structured wage increases to more workers.

Workers are navigating a highly competitive job market where technological disruption is raising performance expectations while traditional job security structures are being redefined.

---

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is directing significant funding and regulatory attention toward transport, digital safety, and workforce development. Singapore will invest S$800 million in transport research and innovation over the next five years under the **Research, Innovation and Enterprise 2030 (RIE2030)** plan. This includes preparing the transport workforce for the transition to autonomous vehicles through specialized career conversion programmes and training incentives.

In the digital space, the **Ministry of Digital Development and Information (MDDI)** is actively gathering public feedback on social media safety. The government has signaled it may block social media platforms for users under 18 if these platforms fail to meet strict child safety standards. Additionally, the **Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA)** is tightening oversight, tasking critical information infrastructure leaders to review risks from AI-enabled cyber threats.

To streamline workforce policy, a new **Skills and Workforce Development Agency** will be established later this year. The government is also tightening regulations on data privacy, proposing mandatory notification requirements for companies that use personal data to train artificial intelligence models.

The state is actively intervening to regulate the digital economy and fund advanced transport infrastructure while building new institutional frameworks to manage workforce transitions.

---

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore's economic relationship with its immediate neighbors is deepening, driven by infrastructure projects and cost differentials. Planning for the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** is nearing completion, with a potential launch in March. This zone, alongside the upcoming **Johor Bahru-Singapore Rapid Transit System (RTS) Link**, is expected to shift significant consumer spending from Singapore to Johor, with some business studies projecting over S$1 billion in annual spending by Singaporeans in Malaysia.

This cost differential is also changing business operations. Local food firms are increasingly shifting their mass production facilities to Malaysia to manage costs, while keeping their research, development, and quality control functions in Singapore.

Regionally, Singapore is competing with Hong Kong to capture the global gold trade, with Singapore building vaulting facilities backed by the **Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS)** to attract central banks. Meanwhile, Indonesia is tightening its regulatory environment for startups, creating some friction for foreign venture capital in the region.

Singapore is positioning itself as the high-value hub of Southeast Asia while its physical and economic integration with Malaysia accelerates.

---

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global geopolitical tensions are directly impacting Singapore's trade and energy security. Ongoing conflict in the Middle East and threats to shipping in the Strait of Hormuz have caused global energy losses and continue to threaten regional supply chains. This instability keeps local fuel and utility costs elevated and highlights the vulnerability of critical undersea communication cables.

The technology sector is also caught in the US-China rivalry. While US restrictions on advanced semiconductor exports continue, Chinese AI firms like **DeepSeek** and **Moonshot AI** are releasing low-cost models that disrupt the global market. This tech stock volatility directly affects Singapore's electronics exports, which, despite a recent surge driven by AI demand, remain highly concentrated and vulnerable to global market shifts.
