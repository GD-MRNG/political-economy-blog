## THE GROUND PICTURE

**Singapore** has reclaimed its position as the world's most competitive economy, driven by strong corporate performance and business efficiency. Yet, this macro-level success contrasts with a daily reality that many residents describe as a high-pressure environment. Local workers are navigating a complex transition marked by rapid artificial intelligence integration, persistent cost pressures, and structural changes in the job market.

At the same time, the physical and digital infrastructure of the city is shifting. Major transit developments like the upcoming **Circle Line** stations at Keppel, Cantonment, and Prince Edward Road promise faster commutes, while older transit systems require increasingly complex maintenance. Meanwhile, the retail and food landscape is churning, with long-standing establishments closing down as businesses adapt to changing consumer habits and rising operational costs.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost stack for Singaporean households remains heavy, even as targeted policy measures attempt to cushion the blow. The **Energy Market Authority** has announced that electricity tariffs will rise significantly starting in July due to Singapore's high reliance on imported natural gas. This increase comes despite the easing of global energy tensions following a preliminary US-Iran peace agreement. To help families manage these rising costs, the government disbursed 500 dollars in **CDC Vouchers** in June, and **FairPrice Group** has frozen prices on over 500 daily essentials until the end of August.

In the property market, the government is maintaining a steady supply of housing, with the **Housing & Development Board** launching over 6,950 Build-To-Order flats in June and planning another 8,000 flats for October in areas like Bayshore and Tengah. While residential rental rates for both HDB flats and private condominiums have eased slightly, property acquisition costs remain high, highlighted by record-setting land bids in prime areas like River Valley. Meanwhile, Certificate of Entitlement prices have seen a broad drop, suggesting a temporary ceiling has been reached in vehicle ownership costs.

The core dynamic at work is a tightly managed domestic economy where targeted government subsidies and housing supply adjustments are running parallel to rising utility and operational costs.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The local labor market continues to show resilience with consecutive quarters of employment growth, but the nature of work is changing rapidly. The widespread adoption of artificial intelligence is causing visible anxiety among professionals, particularly as companies begin to absorb middle management functions through automation. While overall retrenchments have risen, technology and manufacturing sectors are still hiring, with firms increasingly prioritizing adaptability and problem-solving over purely technical coding skills.

In contrast, traditional sectors are experiencing distinct talent strains. The banking sector has seen a significant slowdown in hiring, with headcount growth flatlining, while one in three young lawyers reports a desire to leave the legal profession due to burnout. Freelancers in the nightlife and entertainment sectors are also facing financial insecurity, reporting frequent issues with delayed payments and unpaid dues.

The core dynamic is a bifurcated job market where technical and AI-adjacent roles command salary premiums while traditional corporate and service roles face consolidation and high rates of burnout.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is actively steering the workforce toward an AI-driven economy while strengthening regulatory oversight across multiple sectors. Prime Minister Lawrence Wong announced the formation of the **Tripartite Jobs Council** to protect workers from displacement, supported by the upcoming establishment of a new **Skills and Workforce Development Agency**. To prepare the wider population, the **National Trades Union Congress** has partnered with major cloud providers to offer over one million AI training places.

On the regulatory front, the state is tightening rules around digital safety and corporate governance. The **Cyber Security Agency** has mandated that critical information infrastructure leaders review risks from AI-enabled threats, while parliament has introduced new digital regulations to combat deepfakes and illicit online content. Additionally, the **Competition and Consumer Commission of Singapore** is launching a fast-track procedure in July to resolve business infringement cases more quickly by offering reduced fines for cooperating firms.

The core direction is an aggressive state-led push to build AI capability and digital resilience while simultaneously tightening regulatory guardrails on technology, corporate conduct, and public safety.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its economic integration with its immediate neighbors, even as regional competition intensifies. Planning for the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone** is nearing completion, which local businesses hope will help mitigate the impact of global trade tariffs. To support this cross-border flow, parliament passed a law to manage operational incidents on the upcoming **Rapid Transit System Link**, and a new taxi travel scheme has been implemented between Singapore and Johor.

However, neighboring policy shifts are introducing new variables. Malaysia is set to unify national diesel prices in July to curb smuggling, which will alter logistics costs, while its domestic energy security has been bolstered by new gas field developments in Central Asia. In Indonesia, the temporary suspension of its signature free meal program and fluctuations in the rupiah have created market volatility, occasionally fueling nationalistic social media sentiment directed at Singapore's financial position.

The core dynamic is a push toward deeper physical and economic integration with Malaysia, balanced against volatile domestic policy shifts and market fluctuations in Indonesia.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global geopolitical tensions and supply chain realignments are directly shaping Singapore's economic landscape. The preliminary peace agreement between the US and Iran has reopened the Strait of Hormuz, bringing temporary relief to global shipping routes, but local refiners and energy markets remain highly sensitive to any setbacks in the negotiations. At the same time, the ongoing US-China technology dispute continues to impact local supply chains, particularly as the US restricts foreign access to advanced AI models, prompting companies like **Anthropic** to pull their latest systems from the regional market.

These global disruptions are driving significant capital inflows into Singapore, cementing its status as an Asia-Pacific wealth hub. Institutional capital is flowing into Singapore's listed tech manufacturers to fund the AI infrastructure boom, while local retail investors are increasingly looking at global aerospace and space economy equities as long-term bets.

The core dynamic is Singapore acting as a safe harbor for global capital and technology infrastructure, even as it remains highly vulnerable to international trade restrictions and energy supply shocks.
