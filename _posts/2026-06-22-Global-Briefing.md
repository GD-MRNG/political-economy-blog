# Executive Summary

**The Global Operating Picture**

The international system is experiencing a structural transition characterized by the erosion of Western normative authority and the rise of highly transactional, multipolar alignments. This shift is visible in the divergence between Western security narratives and public sentiment across the Global South, where majorities increasingly identify the United States as a primary threat to regional stability and reject its military footprint. At the same time, the domestic political architectures of the Western core are showing signs of persistent volatility, exemplified by rapid leadership turnover in the United Kingdom and shifting public alignments in the United States. These domestic pressures constrain the ability of Western states to project consistent, long-term strategic commitments, forcing them toward more coercive and extractive diplomatic postures that incentivize both allies and adversaries to seek greater strategic autonomy.

This fragmentation is acutely visible in the global trade and industrial landscape, where the transition from integrated global supply chains to defensive economic blocs is accelerating. The European Union’s preparation for aggressive trade defenses against what it terms a second wave of Chinese industrial imports, alongside expanding US technology and investment controls, has triggered a structural reorganization of capital and production. Rather than producing absolute decoupling, these regulatory barriers are forcing Chinese firms to localize production within Europe and reroute supply chains through third-party intermediaries in Southeast Asia. This complex re-routing allows capital to bypass political barriers, even as Beijing retaliates with targeted export controls and procurement bans on Western firms, signaling a permanent shift toward a more fragmented and legally complex trading environment.

Underlying this geopolitical and economic friction is a critical material tension between technological ambition and resource constraints. The rapid scaling of artificial intelligence and advanced computing is driving an unprecedented demand for physical infrastructure, specialized materials, and energy. This technological race is forcing a structural pivot back to fossil fuel energy sources across East Asia to power data centers, even as states attempt to manage the transition to renewable grids. The immense capital requirements of this physical expansion are testing global liquidity and driving significant public-private investment commitments, while central banks across East Asia raise interest rates to contain the resulting inflationary pressures and manage currency volatility in a highly unstable geopolitical environment.

**Key Strategic Shifts**

* **The diminishing returns of weaponized economic interdependence.** The persistent use of dollar-based sanctions, export controls, and investment restrictions by Western powers is accelerating the development of parallel financial and trade architectures. This is demonstrated by China’s facilitation of domestic listings for foreign-funded firms and the expansion of non-Western transaction networks, which permanently reduce Western regulatory leverage and incentivize global actors to hedge against unilateral financial actions.

* **The material and ecological regression of the technological race.** The immense power requirements of artificial intelligence data centers are driving a structural revival of fossil fuel energy usage, particularly gas-fired power, challenging global decarbonization timelines. This dynamic is forcing states to balance long-term climate commitments against the immediate strategic necessity of securing physical AI infrastructure, leading to massive capital reallocations toward grid stability and hardware supply chains.

* **The reorganization of trade through strategic intermediaries.** In response to rising tariff barriers and investment controls between the US, the EU, and China, global manufacturing is restructuring around neutral intermediary hubs in Southeast Asia and localized production facilities within consumer markets. This shift maintains the flow of goods but increases transaction costs, alters regional labor dynamics, and creates complex regulatory challenges as firms seek to obscure the national origins of critical technologies.

* **The widening legitimacy gap in global security architecture.** The structural divergence between Western security priorities and the strategic interests of the Global South is increasing the diplomatic and logistical costs of Western power projection. As international public opinion increasingly views Western interventions as destabilizing, traditional security alignments are fraying, forcing major powers to rely on highly transactional, short-term arrangements rather than stable, values-based alliances.

# North America <a id='north-america'></a>





<details markdown="1">
<summary><b>Current Assessment</b></summary>

**The Regional Operating Picture**

The North American regional environment is currently defined by a profound structural tension between an increasingly coercive outward security posture and a fragmenting domestic socio-political core. Externally, the United States is transitioning from a grand strategy of hegemonic leadership based on institutional integration and public goods provision toward a model of transactional extraction. Driven by the internal logic of maintaining global primacy amid relative economic decline, Washington increasingly treats both allies and adversaries as targets for economic and political concessions. This shift toward a more predatory posture is designed to shore up domestic industrial capacity and arrest the erosion of American influence. However, by treating traditional alliances as transactional arrangements, this strategy is accelerating the very fragmentation it seeks to prevent, incentivizing partners in Europe and East Asia to hedge against American unreliability by seeking greater strategic autonomy.

This external assertiveness is occurring alongside a deep-seated domestic realignment within the United States, where the material conditions of extreme wealth concentration and persistent inequality are eroding long-standing ideological consensus. The historical barriers that suppressed systemic economic dissent during the Cold War era have largely dissolved, opening a structural space for a fundamental re-evaluation of the capitalist model, particularly among younger demographics. This domestic polarization is further complicated by the visible success of alternative, state-led development models, notably that of China, which challenges the narrative of Western institutional inevitability. Consequently, the domestic political architecture of the regional hegemon is characterized by heightened volatility and a lack of policy continuity, directly undermining the state's capacity to project stable, long-term strategic commitments abroad.

The intersection of these domestic and external dynamics creates a critical structural paradox for North America. To sustain its competitive position against peer adversaries, the United States requires long-term strategic discipline and resource prioritization. Yet, the domestic political landscape is highly volatile, marked by rapid shifts in public alignment and deep skepticism toward foreign entanglements. This mismatch between external strategic ambitions and domestic political constraints is likely to result in highly erratic policy execution. As the domestic consensus continues to fray, the state is forced to rely on increasingly coercive economic instruments—such as unilateral tariffs, investment controls, and financial sanctions—which carry diminishing returns and hasten the development of parallel, non-Western financial architectures.

**Key Strategic Shifts**

* **The self-liquidating feedback loop of coercive economic statecraft.** The accelerating reliance of the United States on unilateral tariffs, investment restrictions, and dollar-based sanctions is driving a structural reorganization of global capital. Rather than isolating adversaries, these measures are incentivizing both rivals and traditional allies to establish parallel financial transaction networks and localized supply chains that bypass Western regulatory jurisdictions. This shift is entering a new phase as these alternative architectures mature, permanently reducing the long-term efficacy of the dollar as a tool of geopolitical leverage.

* **The erosion of the domestic ideological consensus on market capitalism.** Persistent material inequality and wealth concentration within the United States are driving a structural shift in public sentiment, particularly among younger cohorts, toward systemic economic alternatives. This dynamic is transitioning from a diffuse dissatisfaction with economic outcomes to a more structured critique of market mechanisms and a growing interest in state-led or democratic control of production. This internal ideological fragmentation weakens the domestic foundation of American exceptionalism, complicating the state's ability to mobilize public support for external strategic competition.

* **The strategic overextension and prioritization dilemma.** The United States remains structurally entangled in peripheral regional conflicts, particularly in the Middle East and Eastern Europe, which diverts material resources and political capital away from its primary strategic focus on the Indo-Pacific power balance. This persistent overextension, driven in part by domestic interest groups and legacy alliance commitments, is straining military logistics and interceptor stockpiles. This dynamic is accelerating a visible rift between Washington's stated grand strategy of prioritizing the East Asian theater and its actual operational deployments, increasing the risk of strategic exhaustion.
