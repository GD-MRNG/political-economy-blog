## How Events Are Analysed

The starting point is always structure, not surface. Rather than reporting what happened, the analysis asks *why* it was possible — what underlying conditions made an event likely, and what it signals about where things are heading.

A few consistent principles guide the work:

**Material conditions come first.** Politics, policy, and technology are read through their relationship to physical and financial realities — energy flows, supply chains, capital allocation, production costs. Narrative and ideology are treated as secondary, often as a rationalisation of material interests rather than their cause.

**Power is relational.** No actor — state, firm, or institution — is analysed in isolation. The focus is on how leverage is held, exercised, and contested between parties, and how that shapes outcomes more than stated intentions do.

**History sets the baseline.** Current events are situated in longer structural trends. A policy shift or market move that looks sudden usually reflects a tension that has been building for years. Understanding that context is what separates analysis from commentary.

**Scale matters.** A global shift in energy governance lands differently in Singapore than it does in Washington or Riyadh. The analysis tries to trace how macro-level structural changes translate into specific pressures on firms, households, and governments in this part of the world.

The aim is not prediction, but orientation — a clearer picture of the forces at work so that decisions, whether personal, professional, or political, can be made with better information.
