*Political economy is the study of how power and material conditions — capital, energy, production, trade — shape political outcomes, and how political arrangements in turn shape economic life. It treats politics and economics not as separate fields but as two faces of the same reality: who controls what, and what that means for everyone else.*

---

This site publishes analysis of political economy at three scales: the global system, the technology sector, and the lived experience of Singapore and Southeast Asia.

Each report synthesizes key global developments and delivers disciplined analysis across geopolitics, economics, security, and emerging risks. Rather than chasing headlines, the focus is on identifying meaningful shifts, connecting strategic signals, and assessing their broader implications for the international system.

The mission is simple: to document, track, and evaluate consequential global change through consistent, methodical weekly reporting.

## Why This Exists

This blog is part of my study of political economy — and a response to a problem I kept running into.

The world is changing quickly. The structures that governed trade, security, energy, and technology for the past several decades are visibly fracturing. Most people can feel it, but few have been given the tools to make sense of it. That is not a personal failing. It is a gap in how we are taught to understand the world. Most of what passes for public discourse offers ideology or commentary — frameworks that guide you toward certain conclusions without giving you the means to evaluate them independently.

Without structural thinking, you navigate by what others tell you to believe. You can follow events, but you cannot read them. You cannot see the forces underneath, trace the connections, or anticipate where things are heading. It is a bit like living without depth perception — you can see what is in front of you, but you cannot judge distance or direction.

I started putting this work out for two reasons: to sharpen my own understanding, and to offer others a way to widen their perspective. Not to provide answers, but to build the habit of asking better questions — and to make the connections that turn a news cycle into a legible picture of how the world actually works.

## How Events Are Analysed

The starting point is always structure, not surface. Rather than reporting what happened, the analysis asks *why* it was possible — what underlying conditions made an event likely, and what it signals about where things are heading.

A few consistent principles guide the work:

**Material conditions come first.** Politics, policy, and technology are read through their relationship to physical and financial realities — energy flows, supply chains, capital allocation, production costs. Narrative and ideology are treated as secondary, often as a rationalisation of material interests rather than their cause.

**Power is relational.** No actor — state, firm, or institution — is analysed in isolation. The focus is on how leverage is held, exercised, and contested between parties, and how that shapes outcomes more than stated intentions do.

**History sets the baseline.** Current events are situated in longer structural trends. A policy shift or market move that looks sudden usually reflects a tension that has been building for years. Understanding that context is what separates analysis from commentary.

**Scale matters.** A global shift in energy governance lands differently in Singapore than it does in Washington or Riyadh. The analysis tries to trace how macro-level structural changes translate into specific pressures on firms, households, and governments in this part of the world.

The aim is not prediction, but orientation — a clearer picture of the forces at work so that decisions, whether personal, professional, or political, can be made with better information.

## On reading the world clearly without being broken by it

You are here because you are paying attention.

You read the briefings. You track the disruptions. You understand, broadly, that the world is undergoing one of its periodic restructurings and that the old maps are less reliable than they used to be.

That kind of attention is valuable. But it comes with a cost the briefings themselves don't discuss: if you absorb the weight of the world's instability without a way of carrying it, the information eventually becomes either paralyzing or numbing. You freeze under the scale of it, or you develop a protective cynicism that keeps everything at arm's length. Neither of those is the same as understanding.

There is a third way.

### The Two Bad Options

The world tends to offer two responses and dress them both up as wisdom.

The first is compliance — let the urgency set your tempo, keep pace, adapt constantly, optimise. This feels productive. But the treadmill always calls for faster, and there is no point at which it declares you adequate and lets you rest.

The second is withdrawal — detach, disengage, maintain a knowing distance from a world too compromised to deserve your full investment. This feels honest. But it is a sophisticated despair, and despair, however clear-eyed, is still a form of flight.

### The Third Thing

What most people are quietly reaching for is harder and more honest than either of those.

It is the capacity to hold two things at once: that the world is genuinely troubled, *and* that it is genuinely worth living in. Not as a compromise, not by blurring one into the other — but by refusing to let either cancel out the other.

Call it lucid affection. Loving the world without being credulous about it. The clarity and the warmth are not in conflict. Each makes the other more honest — the warmth keeps the clarity from curdling into despair, and the clarity keeps the warmth from tipping into delusion.

This is not a new idea. Marcus Aurelius governed a declining empire through plagues and wars and wrote, in private journals never meant to be read, with genuine warmth about the simple fact of being alive. He did not wait for the external situation to improve before allowing himself to notice what was still good. Laozi described the same posture differently: stay soft, move with the grain of things, conserve yourself so that when real action is required you meet it from fullness rather than depletion.

### A Note to the Reader

The information on this site is here to help you understand what is happening. But understanding what is happening is not the same as carrying it at all times.

Stay informed. Read the hard things. Keep clear eyes.

And then return to your life — the people you care about, the things you find beautiful, the ordinary texture of your days — not in spite of knowing what you know, but with it. Those things are not less real than the briefings. In many ways they are more real. They are the actual substance of the life you are living, not merely the conditions surrounding it.

The world is genuinely troubled. It is also genuinely worth living in.

*You do not have to choose.*
